Bring your child in for

  • First dental visit (by the first birthday)
  • White or brown spots on baby teeth
  • Complaints of pain while eating
  • Fluoride & sealant protection before cavities start
  • A knocked or chipped tooth from play
  • Thumb-sucking or late tooth questions

What happens, step by step

  1. Hello first

    The first minutes are just talking, showing the chair, counting teeth with a mirror — building trust before anything else.

  2. Gentle examination

    We check teeth, gums and how the bite is developing, explaining to the parent as we go.

  3. Prevention

    Fluoride varnish strengthens enamel; sealants coat the chewing grooves where most kids' cavities start.

  4. Treatment if needed

    Small fillings or a baby-tooth pulpectomy, done slowly, with breaks whenever your child needs one.

Aftercare

  • Praise the visit — your calm is contagious
  • Brush twice daily together until age 8
  • Limit sticky sweets to mealtimes, not grazing
  • Six-monthly check-ups keep visits short and fear-free

About our doctors

Common questions

When should my child first see a dentist?

By their first birthday, or within six months of the first tooth. Early visits are short, friendly and mostly about prevention and comfort.

Why treat baby teeth if they fall out anyway?

Baby teeth hold space for adult teeth, and infections in them hurt and spread. Treating small problems early also prevents a lifetime of dental fear.

My child is terrified of dentists. What do you do?

We go at their pace — a first visit may be just a chair ride and counting teeth. No forcing, no surprises, parents stay in the room.

Are sealants worth it?

Yes — they're one of dentistry's best-proven preventions, sealing the grooves where most childhood cavities begin, for a fraction of a filling's cost.
